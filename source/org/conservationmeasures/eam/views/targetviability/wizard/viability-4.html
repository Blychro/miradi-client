The next step is to develop one or more indicators for each KEA.

To create a new indicator, make sure the KEA is selected in the upper panel, and click the <Create Indicator> button.  Fill in the following fields:

  • Indicator: The name of the indicator. Generally 2-5 words (e.g., Number of Sharks).
  • Short Label: The text that will appear in the indicator symbol on the diagram. Often a number (e.g., "3" or "17").

Click on the priority and progress boxes to assign the appropriate level to each indicator.

To edit or delete an indicator, click on the indicator in the upper panel, and then either edit the fields in the lower panel, or click on the <Delete Indicator> button.



 


Although key ecological attributes are specific descriptions of an aspect of a target, they are generally still too broad to measure or assess in a cost-effective manner over time. To this end, it is important to develop indicators that can be used to assess the attribute over time. An indicator, in simplest terms, is what you measure to keep track of the status of a key ecological attribute.

In many cases an indicator can be the same as the attribute itself. For example, if your attribute is population size, the indicator may be the number of individuals in the population if you can count this number directly. If you cannot count this number directly, then your indicator will specify how you will measure this number – for example, for a fish population, as catch per unit effort using a specific technique at a given time of the year.

In other cases, however, developing a good indicator will require a bit more thinking to find a way of measuring the attribute over time. For example, if your attribute is the water quality of a stream, it is not possible to measure every physical and chemical parameter. Instead, you would select a few representative parameters – for example water temperature and oxygen levels – that you feel can represent the overall water quality. You can also combine several measurable properties into a composite indicator or index.

Indicators frequently involve some type of quantitative assessment – such as number of acres, recruitment rate, age class sizes, percent of cover, or frequency of fire of a given intensity. Other indicators may involve measurable elements that are not numerical, such as the seasonality of fire or flooding.

Indicators can be assigned different priorities as follows:

  • Very High: Must be monitored
  • High: High priority for monitoring
  • Medium: Moderate priority for monitoring
  • Low: Monitor only if resources allow

You can record the current progress in monitoring each indicator as follows:

  • Fully on Schedule: Monitoring going as planned or better
  • Going Well: Monitoring generally progressing
  • Problems Implementing: Monitoring encountering some challenges
  • Not Started: Little or no work done to date
