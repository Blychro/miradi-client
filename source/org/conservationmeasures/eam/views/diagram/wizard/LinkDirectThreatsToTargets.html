1C. Understand Project Context:

Link Your Direct Threats to Targets

Many threats affect more than one target. To link your threat to another target, select the direct threat, hold the <Shift> key and then select the desired target. Then click on the <Add a Link> button (the arrow).

You can also link direct threats to each other using the <Add a Link> button.

If desired, you can also show a stress between a direct threat and a target. On a link connecting a direct threat with a target, double-click or right-click and choose Properties, and enter the stress name where prompted.

Many conservation projects can adequately capture the complexity of their situation by showing direct threats and targets. Some projects, however, need to show how a specific direct threat affects a target. A stress is a degraded attribute of a conservation targets that is caused by human activities (e.g., low population size, reduced extent of forest system; reduced river flows; increased sedimentation; lowered groundwater table level).

Hint: To keep your model reasonably simple and to make it an effective communication tool, it is suggested to show the major links between direct threats and targets. If you have a direct threat that has a negligible impact on a target, you should consider not including that relationship in your model.

Remember, building your model is as much an art as a science. It will not be perfect, but it should do a reasonable job of representing what is happening at your site.
